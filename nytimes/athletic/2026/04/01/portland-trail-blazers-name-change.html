Portland franchise to retire "Trail Blazers" name under terms of Dundon ownership deal

Prospective owner Tom Dundon made retirement of the name a condition of his purchase agreement after a review concluded that "Trail Blazers" was too closely tied to colonial-era frontier mythology and westward expansion.

By Jason Quick Published 8:12 a.m. EST Updated 9:41 a.m. EST Portland, Ore.

The Portland NBA franchise will retire the “Trail Blazers” name at the conclusion of the 2025-26 season, according to two league sources briefed on the decision and one team executive familiar with a final recommendation delivered to prospective owner Tom Dundon in February as a condition of his pending acquisition of the club. That recommendation concluded the name's connection to frontier conquest and settler expansion had become too central to the brand to defend publicly.

Sources with direct knowledge of the sale process said Dundon, a Carolina-based businessman best known as the majority owner of the NHL's Carolina Hurricanes, insisted on a formal review of the name's colonial associations as a non-negotiable term of his purchase agreement. The review was subsequently led by team strategy staff, outside brand consultants, and league representatives. In a draft statement reviewed by The Athletic, club leadership said the existing name had been reevaluated because its language "romanticizes westward expansion in a way that cannot be separated from colonial displacement."

"This is not about erasing history. It is about acknowledging that 'Trail Blazers' carries a colonial frame rooted in conquest, movement into occupied land, and a version of the Northwest that many people no longer want the franchise to celebrate," one senior team official said on the condition of anonymity, as the public rollout has not yet been scheduled.

People with knowledge of the process said Dundon's representatives signed off on the final direction late last week, clearing the way for a phased transition that will begin with digital channels in June and continue through uniforms, court markings, and arena signage before opening night next season. The sale itself, still awaiting league ratification, is not expected to be delayed by the timing of the rebrand rollout.

What happens next

  • Late April: final legal clearance and trademark filings.
  • Early June: digital identity transition across team channels.
  • Mid July: full brand reveal event in downtown Portland.
  • Preseason: court, jersey, and arena mark conversion complete.

League comissioner Adam Silver declined to discuss specific franchises but said the NBA has worked with multiple clubs in recent years on "language standards and brand context" as part of its wider community-affairs framework. The players' association did not immediately respond to a request for comment.

The franchise has used the Trail Blazers name since joining the league in 1970. According to people involved in the review, outside advisers warned that the name's longstanding marketing around pioneers, trails, and frontier ambition made its colonial implications unusually difficult to soften through partial rebranding alone. Several replacement options remain under consideration, including names tied to regional geography and transportation history. Officials said the final selection is expected to be announced at a public event after the draft lottery.

One longtime employee who attended internal briefings described the mood inside the organization as "nervous but energized," noting that leadership has framed the move as a long-term cultural reset rather than a one-season marketing initiative.

Team president Dewayne Hankins said in a brief written comment that the organization has "spent months listening carefully" to season-ticket holders, community partners, and current players. Hankins did not confirm the exact replacement name but an anonymous source with close ties to leadership said that the current frontrunner is the Portland "Nonbinary Allies".
